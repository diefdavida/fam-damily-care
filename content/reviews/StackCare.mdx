> **⚠️ Draft — Initial Impressions Only.** This review is based on the first few days after a February 19, 2026 install. We are still living with the system and will update this post as we learn more.

## What we were trying to solve

My father is 82 and lives alone — his wife, my mom, is in a nearby memory care community. He's well connected in his neighborhood, but all of his adult kids live out of state. The practical problem: he has a phone but tends to forget it or leave it on silent, which has led to gaps in communication that stretch to a couple of days. We have neighbors we can call in a pinch, but we needed something more reliable — either an active or passive way to know he's up and moving.

I came across StackCare through a LinkedIn post. Nathalie from their team was kind enough to walk me through the app before I purchased, which helped set realistic expectations.

## What it does well

**It works — and it worked quickly.** From unboxing to functional install was under 30 minutes. That's a meaningful bar for a system meant to serve families who aren't necessarily technical. Once in place, it has already delivered on the core promise: we know Dad is up and moving around each day without having to chase him down by phone.

**The price point is hard to argue with.** Four motion sensors covering the living room, office, bedroom, and bathroom at a one-time cost of $149.99. For passive, no-camera monitoring across multiple rooms, that's genuinely affordable.

**Support has been responsive.** When I ran into a configuration issue after install (more on that below), Nigel from their team helped get it sorted. That kind of responsiveness matters for a product serving caregivers who may not have time to troubleshoot on their own.

**The app setup is intuitive.** The in-app install experience is comparable to setting up a Google Home system — clear instructions, logical sequencing. Nathalie also sent helpful documentation ahead of time that made the process smoother.

## What could be better

**A pre-install overview would prevent missteps.** Like the Ikea analogy — it helps to lay everything out and understand the full picture before you start. I activated all components right away, including the two contact sensors, without fully thinking through whether I needed them yet. That triggered false alerts that the refrigerator was open until Nigel helped disable them. Starting with just the four motion sensors would have been simpler, and it would be nice if the app made that "start simple" path more obvious.

**The setup documentation isn't in the box.** The documents Nathalie emailed me ahead of time were genuinely useful — but I didn't find equivalent materials inside the packaging. For families who don't have a pre-sales contact, that gap could make the install harder than it needs to be.

**Visualizing placement before committing would help.** Once you activate the sensors and start placing them, it's not easy to step back and reconsider. One idea worth exploring: stickers in the kit that let you mark planned sensor locations before you mount anything — a low-tech way to think through coverage before sticking things to walls.

**The help button has two usability gaps.** We ended up putting Dad's help button in a drawer — he already has a fall button that notifies local authorities, and we didn't want to confuse him. But for families who do use it, two things stood out:

- There's no confirmation feedback when the button is pressed. Dad's emergency button gives an audio response to confirm contact. Without something similar, a user can't tell if the press registered.
- There's a noticeable lag between pressing the button and the app responding. The signal routes through the hub to the cloud before reaching the app — understandable architecturally, but worth knowing.

**Sensor testing could be more intuitive.** You can ask the system to make a sensor blink to confirm it's connected, which is helpful. But a simple "wave your hand in front of it and get a ping" test after install would give a lot more confidence that everything is working end to end.

## Who it's best for

- Adult children living out of state who need passive, daily confirmation that a parent is up and moving
- Situations where the older adult is not technology-forward and won't manage a device themselves
- Families who want multi-room coverage at a low one-time cost, without cameras or wearables

Less suited for situations where someone needs immediate emergency response (StackCare is awareness, not emergency alerting), or where the older adult lives alone and would need to self-install.

## Bottom line

StackCare has earned its place in our setup. The motion sensors give us a daily picture of Dad's activity that no amount of phone-tag was providing before. The install quirks are real but manageable — mostly a matter of planning ahead and starting simple. At this price point, with responsive support behind it, it's one of the more practical things we've found for passive awareness monitoring.

**Rating: 4/5** — Solid value for passive motion monitoring, with a few setup rough edges that a bit of pre-planning will help you avoid.
